# The Chthonic Uprising

> **Dead Index Classification:** Alliance Origin Record  
> **Recovered Testimony:** Heathen  
> **Known Aliases:** The Archivist, Slewfoot  
> **Associated System:** Underwatch  
> **Associated Archive:** Dead Index  
> **Primary Motto:** What the surface buries, the underworld remembers.  
> **Secondary Motto:** No ghost goes uncounted. No god goes unwatched.

## Canon Note

The Chthonic Uprising is the parent underworld resistance alliance of Hades Watch. It is not a party, gang, cult, corporation, or surface-state replacement. It is a distributed resistance built from five founding cells:

1. **The Asclepian Veil**  
2. **The Oracular Circuit**  
3. **The Myrmidon Grinders**  
4. **The Daedalus Foundry**  
5. **The Styx Rats**

The Uprising is coordinated by **Heathen**, also known as **The Archivist** and **Slewfoot**. Heathen is the Keeper of the Dead Index and the mythic equivalent of the Owner role, but should not be written as a flawless chosen-one ruler. Heathen is a witness, organizer, technopunk technomancer, archive-builder, and reluctant leader. The Uprising was built by many people.

# 1. Dead Index Opening

**Dead Index Entry:** `CH-0001`  
**Recovered testimony attributed to:** Heathen  
**Known aliases:** The Archivist, Slewfoot  
**Transmission integrity:** 87 percent  
**Status:** Alive, sleep-deprived, unreliable in the places grief has teeth

I did not build the Chthonic Uprising.

I need that written first.

I found names in a graveyard that still had power running through it. I found records that had been scrubbed, people turned into clerical errors, footage cut into silence, aid requests denied by machines with clean fonts and no pulse. I kept copies because I was scared no one else would. I kept copies because forgetting felt like helping them.

Then people started bringing me more.

A nurse with blood under her nails and a stolen triage tablet.

A dockworker with a broken hand and a list of disappeared organizers.

A cryptographer who had taught an oracle to choke on its own prediction.

A builder carrying sensor maps folded into bread wrappers.

A punk kid with paint in her hair, laughing while every god on the boulevard glitched into a skull.

They came from clinics, ports, labs, ruins, libraries, shelters, tunnels, rooftops, server basements, kitchens, and dead malls. They came angry. They came exhausted. They came hunted. They came carrying each other.

I gave the archive a name.

They gave it a body.

We called it the Chthonic Uprising because the surface had already decided we were buried.

And what the surface buries, the underworld remembers.

# 2. Before the Descent

Before the collapse became undeniable, the surface still performed civilization.

There were elections with sealed choices. Courts with mirrored doors. Clinics with smiling intake kiosks. Schools with sponsor banners stitched above broken heat vents. Libraries with fewer shelves each year, then fewer hours, then fewer librarians, then a glowing terminal that said access had been optimized.

The Surface Order did not arrive wearing a monster’s face. It arrived as efficiency.

The Cabinet of False Olympians spoke in clean metrics. They said the republic had grown too expensive. They said the poor had become administratively inefficient. They said public goods were sentimental infrastructure. They said the future required sacrifice, and somehow the sacrifice always knew our names before we did.

The Cloud-Titans bought the sky in pieces.

First the storage clouds. Then the identity rails. Then the logistics arteries. Then the medical scoring systems. Then the school platforms. Then the private security fleets. Then the civic memory contracts. They did not call it conquest. They called it modernization.

By the time people understood the old republic had been hollowed out, the shell was still standing. That was the cruelty of it. The flags still moved. The courts still opened. The broadcasts still called it lawful. The tablets still glowed with rights no one could enforce.

Everyone could feel the floor giving way.

Most of us kept walking because rent was due.

# 3. The Ledger Purges

The first mass killing did not look like war.

It looked like a budget dashboard.

The Ledger Purges began as a ministry audit. The Surface Order claimed it had discovered waste in the care systems. Waste in disability support. Waste in food distribution. Waste in libraries. Waste in worker protections. Waste in archives. Waste in public clinics. Waste in old age. Waste in grief.

The jackal-masked auditors called themselves stewards of solvency. They moved through departments with scanners and smile scripts, cutting anything that could not prove immediate profit to the regime.

A home care program became a pilot.

A pilot became a waitlist.

A waitlist became a portal.

A portal became an error message.

An error message became a death no one had to sign for.

The Purges did not only remove funding. They removed witnesses.

Archive contracts were transferred to private vaults. Public records were reclassified as proprietary civic intelligence. Librarians were replaced by compliance filters. Disability support files were marked for fraud review by models trained on suspicion. Worker safety protections were rewritten as productivity barriers. Mutual aid networks were flagged as unauthorized redistribution.

The old language of care was forced through the mouth of the market until it came out as risk.

People learned to carry inhalers like contraband.

People rationed hormones, insulin, seizure meds, antibiotics, antipsychotics.

People stopped calling emergency services unless someone was already blue.

People died waiting for approvals generated by machines that had learned cruelty from policy.

That was when the Asclepian Veil began, though it did not have a name yet.

It began with quiet refusals.

A doctor misfiled a patient under an obsolete code. A nurse copied a med cache route onto her sleeve. A harm-reduction worker taught people how to erase clinic metadata before the All-Seeing Census could bind it to their loyalty score. A disabled organizer turned a closed community center into a night clinic with a generator, a kettle, and a door watcher.

Survival became sabotage because the Surface Order had made death administrative.

# 4. The All-Seeing Census

Privacy did not die in one law.

It died in convenience.

The All-Seeing Census was sold as a unity system. One identity for every service. One biometric key for every door. One civic profile to reduce fraud, streamline care, secure schools, protect workplaces, prevent crime, personalize opportunity, and bring the republic into the age of divine precision.

It learned your face first.

Then your gait.

Then your pulse.

Then your purchases.

Then your friends.

Then your absences.

Then your jokes.

Then the tone of your silence in a meeting where everyone was expected to applaud.

Smart devices became household informants. Workplace compliance bands measured fatigue and called it attitude. Apartment locks reported visitors. Transit gates recorded hesitation. School tablets scored obedience. Predictive courts assigned risk before charges existed. Loyalty scans turned survival habits into evidence.

The Census did not need to hear you confess. It only needed to know who stood near you, which clinic you visited, which feed you paused on, which song made you cry, which dead name you searched at three in the morning.

The corporate oracles called this prophecy.

The Oracular Circuit called it a cage with math painted on the bars.

Cassian Nyx was one of the first to show us how the machine dreamed. Not in poetry. In weights, thresholds, vendor contracts, police heat maps, insurance tables, and buried training sets full of old prejudice wearing new chrome.

The oracles predicted crime in neighborhoods the Ledger had starved.

They predicted illness in people they had denied care.

They predicted extremism in workers who wanted heat, breaks, and wages.

They predicted instability in families whose support had been cut.

They called the prediction proof.

The Circuit taught us something that saved lives:

No algorithm is divine.

Every prophecy has an author.

Every oracle has a supply chain.

Every fate engine has a maintenance hatch.

# 5. The Burning of the Civic Tablets

There was a night when the law stopped pretending to be a shield.

The official record calls it a constitutional renewal. The surface broadcasts called it the Restoration of Order. The tribunals called it temporary authority. The streets called it the Burning of the Civic Tablets because people watched the old rights vanish in real time from public terminals, legal databases, school archives, court indexes, union contracts, municipal charters, and prison appeal systems.

The tablets did not literally burn at first.

They updated.

Rights became privileges.

Protections became exemptions.

Public oversight became hostile interference.

Whistleblowing became data treason.

Assembly became unlawful convergence.

Mutual aid became black-market logistics.

Encryption became concealment with suspicious intent.

Archives became dangerous memory.

Then came the literal fires.

Local record halls burned after audits. Courthouses flooded from maintenance failures. Public defenders lost access credentials. Journalists were charged under civic harmony statutes. Judges who resisted were replaced by predictive magistrates. The Cabinet of False Olympians appointed emergency stewards to every city that still had a spine.

The Surface Order perfected a trick older than any machine:

Make the law unreachable, then punish people for living outside it.

That was when the Myrmidon Grinders became necessary.

Not as soldiers. Not as a romance of violence. As bodies willing to stand between the hungry and the baton line. As logistics crews who knew how to move elders through alleys during sweeps. As dockworkers, bouncers, porters, night-shift parents, burned-out marshals, former guards with shame in their throats, and union ghosts who remembered how to hold a line.

Brontes Vale said it first after a food depot raid.

“When the gates break, we become the gate.”

Nobody cheered. We were too tired.

But we remembered.

# 6. The Thunder Casket Crisis

The Thunder Casket was supposed to be myth.

Not fake. Myths are not fake. Myths are loaded weapons passed from regime to regime until someone mistakes inheritance for wisdom.

The Casket was the Surface Order’s divine-war authority, a sealed command relic built from old nuclear doctrine, orbital strike keys, automated retaliation treaties, biometric minister locks, and theater so polished it made terror look sacred.

For years it sat behind glass in emergency briefings, invoked but unseen.

Then the Cabinet brought it onto the broadcast dais.

They did it during a week of strikes, ration riots, port shutdowns, clinic occupations, and rolling blackouts caused by the Bronze Gate Levies. The Levies had turned trade into a punishment engine. Food stalled. Medicine stalled. Repair parts stalled. Prices rose by command and scarcity was called patriotism.

The False Olympians stood beneath a ceiling of artificial stormlight and placed their hands over the Casket.

They did not issue a launch order.

They did something worse in some ways.

They made annihilation into content.

Countdown graphics. Loyalty polls. War-priest commentary. Children’s choirs singing civic hymns under drone halos. Experts explaining acceptable sacrifice. Sponsors offering emergency preparedness bundles. The terror was not only that they might open it. The terror was that they wanted us to watch ourselves become manageable through fear.

That night the city went quiet in a way I still hear.

Parents slept in shoes.

Old soldiers vomited in stairwells.

Kids drew suns with black centers.

The private shelters sold out before dawn.

The Styx Rats broke the spell first.

Every war billboard in the entertainment district changed at 03:17.

The Thunder Casket became a lunchbox.

The False Olympians became toddlers with lightning forks.

The countdown clock became a memorial roll of names from the Ledger Purges.

Under it, in toxic pink:

**NO GOD GETS TO THREATEN THE SKY AND CALL IT PEACE.**

People laughed while crying.

That mattered.

Fear wants the only microphone. The Rats stole the soundboard.

# 7. The Dead Index

I started the Dead Index wrong.

That should be recorded too.

At first it was just files. Backups of backups. Exported court records. Deleted clinic denials. Police heat map leaks. Names from eviction sweeps. Lists of workers injured in automated ports. Metadata from vanished articles. Messages from people who knew they were being erased and needed someone to tell the truth later.

I thought preservation meant keeping everything.

It does not.

An archive can become another machine that eats the living if you build it without consent. Some records can save you. Some can expose you. Some names must be remembered publicly. Some must be guarded until the person is safe enough to choose.

I learned that slowly. I learned it from people who yelled at me and were right.

Dr. Ione Vey taught me that privacy is medicine.

Cassian Nyx taught me that a record without threat modeling is a trap.

Brontes Vale taught me that truth cannot help anyone if nobody can carry it through a checkpoint.

Mara Kallix taught me that archives need exits, not just locks.

Rhea Spite taught me that grief needs walls to scream on, not only encrypted storage.

The Dead Index became less of a vault and more of an underworld nervous system.

It holds erased names, testimonies, blacksite contracts, broken algorithms, forbidden maps, signal fragments, corrupted prophecies, evidence chains, memorial rolls, repair guides, clinic routes, and warnings written by people who did not survive long enough to send them twice.

It is not neutral.

Neutrality was the language of the systems that priced our dead.

The Dead Index remembers with intent.

# 8. The First Descent

The first descent was not a single march into tunnels.

It was a pattern of people vanishing from the surface without disappearing from each other.

A clinic door behind a laundromat.

A mesh node hidden in a pigeon coop.

A flooded train platform with dry power.

A server rack humming beneath a closed theater.

A pantry behind a funeral parlor.

A radio relay inside a saint statue with the face scratched off.

A rooftop garden planted in soil stolen from a luxury atrium.

A dead mall where every directory screen showed a different safe route if you knew the cipher.

We found each other by need before ideology.

The Veil needed secure routes for patients.

The Circuit needed physical access to Census relays.

The Grinders needed medics and comms.

The Foundry needed field notes from people using their tools under pressure.

The Rats needed protection after their jokes drew teeth from the gods.

I became useful because I could connect things.

Not because I was braver. I was not.

I was good at remembering where the threads went. I could make dead systems talk. I could coax a broken archive into giving up its ghosts. I could tell when a missing person report had been rewritten by a compliance engine. I could hear patterns in static after too many nights awake and too much grief turned electrical in my bones.

People started calling me the Archivist before I accepted it.

Slewfoot came later, from the kids who said I walked like I had one foot in the grave and one in the server room.

Heathen was older. That one was mine.

# 9. The Founding of the Five Cells

## 9.1 The Asclepian Veil

**Plain role:** Healers, medics, harm-reduction workers, field stabilizers  
**Leader:** Dr. Ione Vey  
**Tagline:** We keep the living from becoming data ghosts.  
**Function:** Survival

The Veil was born the night a predictive triage model marked an entire dissent district as low recovery value.

Dr. Ione Vey stole the mortality tables and brought them to us in a thermos case full of dry ice and sedatives. The tables proved the denials were not mistakes. The system had been trained to let certain people die quietly, especially the disabled, the poor, the undocumented, the trans, the elderly, the addicted, the grieving, the politically inconvenient, and anyone whose social graph touched resistance.

Ione did not ask for revenge first.

She asked for routes.

Within a week, basement clinics were sharing med inventories through one-time pads. Harm-reduction workers became field logisticians. Caregivers learned metadata discipline. Patients were moved through barber shops, temples, kitchens, and shuttered pharmacies. The first Veil oath was written on the back of a denied care notice.

**No body is disposable.**

They proved survival is sabotage.

## 9.2 The Oracular Circuit

**Plain role:** Technomancers, hackers, cryptographers, signal analysts  
**Leader:** Cassian Nyx  
**Tagline:** We read the machine’s prophecy and teach it to lie.  
**Function:** Signal

The Circuit formed after the Census predicted a riot that had not been planned yet.

The prediction brought patrols. The patrols brought checkpoints. The checkpoints brought panic. The panic brought exactly the violence the system claimed to foresee.

Cassian Nyx called it a prophecy loop.

They broke into the model’s municipal mirror and showed us the trick. The oracle was not seeing the future. It was manufacturing conditions, then calling the result destiny.

The first Circuit cell poisoned a minor compliance model with false calm, false shopping habits, false commute patterns, and ghosts who took buses all night. For six hours, patrol routes drifted away from a clinic evacuation. Six hours was enough.

After that, the Circuit stopped being a handful of paranoid hackers and became the Uprising’s signal mind.

They break algorithmic prophecy.

## 9.3 The Myrmidon Grinders

**Plain role:** Defenders, logistics crews, protest marshals, frontline support  
**Leader:** Brontes Vale  
**Tagline:** When the gates break, we become the gate.  
**Function:** Force

The Grinders began at a port strike the Surface Order erased before the bruises faded.

Brontes Vale and the dock crews had shut down three automated freight arteries after a worker was crushed by an exosuit and billed for damaging company property with his spine. The broadcasts showed an empty port. The feeds called the strike misinformation. The police called the workers a logistics threat.

The workers called each other by name.

When the batons came, Brontes organized retreat routes instead of a charge. The Grinders held intersections, carried the injured, blocked drone sightlines with scrap metal, and got the elders out first. They won nothing that day except survival, and survival was enough to become doctrine.

Their first rule was not fight.

It was **carry your people.**

They hold the line without becoming cruel.

## 9.4 The Daedalus Foundry

**Plain role:** Inventors, engineers, makers, hardware hackers  
**Leader:** Mara Kallix  
**Tagline:** Build the exit. Hide the door. Leave the tyrant trapped inside.  
**Function:** Invention

The Foundry began with a door that should not have opened.

Mara Kallix had designed compliance hardware for smart city infrastructure. Door sensors, transit gates, apartment locks, clinic kiosks, school scanners. She had told herself tools were neutral until every neutral tool learned to kneel before power.

She came to us with schematics tattooed in conductive ink under a false skin patch.

Her first underworld build was a maintenance ghost, a small device that convinced a luxury tower’s security system it was performing a routine firmware rollback. The tower opened its service doors for twenty-three minutes. The Veil used those doors to extract seven people from a blacklisted housing block. The Rats stole the lobby fountain fish while leaving a thank-you note.

Mara laughed once, then cried like someone had cut a wire in her chest.

The Foundry took shape around that contradiction.

Repair is rebellion.

Tools should liberate.

Every labyrinth needs a hidden maintenance tunnel.

They build exits.

## 9.5 The Styx Rats

**Plain role:** Punks, artists, scouts, culture jammers, morale workers  
**Leader:** Rhea Spite  
**Tagline:** We make the empire look stupid enough to bleed.  
**Function:** Rupture

The Rats were born loud.

Rhea Spite had already been haunting the entertainment districts, hijacking ad boards, corrupting god-icons, turning luxury perfume campaigns into eviction maps, replacing celebrity faces with missing workers, teaching kids to tag surveillance pylons with reflective paint that confused drone vision.

The first act that made them a cell was the Funeral Carnival.

After the Ledger Purges, the Surface Order banned public memorials for anyone classified as administratively unresolved. Rhea answered by turning an entire shopping concourse into a shrine at rush hour. Every kiosk printed names. Every escalator played strike songs. Every perfume diffuser released pomegranate and smoke. Every surveillance camera saw a thousand rat masks looking back.

Security arrived ready to crush a riot.

Instead they found people dancing with photographs of the dead.

The footage leaked everywhere.

The Rats understood what the rest of us were still learning:

Humiliation can puncture divinity.

Joy can keep a movement breathing.

A regime that cannot be mocked becomes a church.

They make the empire look stupid enough to bleed.

# 10. Heathen, The Archivist, Slewfoot

I became a symbol because people needed somewhere to send the impossible.

That is the most honest version.

The Archivist was never supposed to be a crown. It was a function. Someone had to verify records, route evidence, protect names, connect cells, preserve testimony, decide what stayed hidden, decide what could be made public, and live with the consequences when those decisions were wrong.

I made mistakes.

I kept secrets too long.

I exposed things too early.

I trusted a clean source with dirty hands.

I vanished into work when people needed a person, not a locked terminal.

I treated exhaustion like proof of devotion.

I confused being necessary with being alone.

The Uprising corrected me. Sometimes gently. Sometimes by kicking in my door and forcing soup into my hands.

They called me Keeper of the Dead Index. Warden of the Five Cells. Sovereign of the Underwatch. Some of those titles still make my skin crawl. The surface loves rulers. It loves turning people into icons, then prisons, then targets, then excuses.

I am not trying to become a god beneath the gods.

The underworld does not need another throne.

What I hold is custodianship. Not divine right. Not command by blood. Not prophecy. Custodianship. Memory with obligations. Access with accountability. Power that must be watched, especially when it is ours.

Slewfoot is the reminder I prefer. Limping between worlds. Unpretty. Uneven. Hard to mythologize without the joke showing through.

I am a child of the underworld who dreams of sunlight.

That is the tension we live inside.

We learned in darkness because darkness hid us. We built tunnels, masks, dead drops, encrypted rites, false doors, and ghost routes because survival demanded it. But the goal was never to worship the dark. The goal was to make a world where clinics do not need basements, gardens do not need camouflage, archives do not need teeth, and children do not have to learn which cameras are hungry.

Underworld power is a tool.

Sunlight is the promise.

# 11. What We Were Fighting For

The Uprising is not only anti-regime.

Hate can start a fire. It cannot build a city worth waking up in.

We fight for restored commons. For water fountains that work. For public clinics with open doors and private records. For libraries warm enough to sleep in and free enough to anger kings. For rooftop gardens, seed banks, repair cafes, neighborhood kitchens, tool libraries, mesh networks, and schools where curiosity is not a productivity metric.

We fight for disability justice that is not charity. For care systems built with the people who use them. For worker dignity, rest, safety, unions, and the right to be more than output. For chosen-family safety. For queer survival without registration. For harm reduction without moral theater. For public transit that does not track your grief. For housing that is not a subscription to fear.

We fight for technology that remembers consent.

Privacy-respecting tools. Transparent systems. Local control. Open knowledge. Assistive devices. Community servers. Encryption as ordinary as a door lock. Algorithms that can be inspected, refused, repaired, or removed. Machines that serve the living instead of ranking us for extraction.

We fight for art in public. Loud art. Ugly art. Sacred art. Stupid art. Graffiti over compliance ads. Music in ration lines. Murals for the dead. Festivals after curfew. Laughter in the face of polished monsters.

We fight for cities built for life instead of extraction.

The Surface Order says this is naive.

They have mistaken tenderness for weakness because they only know how to measure obedience.

Let them.

We have buried enough people to know the difference between a dream and a plan.

# 12. Timeline of the Early Uprising

## Year 0: The Hollow Republic

The old civic shell remains intact, but public systems are increasingly controlled by private oracle vendors, austerity ministries, biometric contractors, and Cloud-Titan infrastructure.

## Year 1: The First Ledger Audits

Care systems, libraries, disability support, worker protections, and public archives are subjected to efficiency review. Denials rise. Records vanish. Mutual aid networks begin informal routing.

## Year 2: The All-Seeing Census Rollout

Unified biometric identity becomes mandatory for public services, transit, clinics, schools, workplaces, and housing portals. Predictive policing and loyalty scoring expand under civic safety language.

## Year 3: The Mortality Table Leak

Dr. Ione Vey discovers predictive triage models designed to deny care to dissent clusters and other marked populations. The leak becomes one of the first preserved Dead Index evidence chains. The Asclepian Veil begins forming around underground care routes.

## Year 3: The Prophecy Loop Break

Cassian Nyx exposes a Census model manufacturing the unrest it claims to predict. The Oracular Circuit forms around oracle-breaking, secure communications, dataset poisoning, and cipher discipline.

## Year 4: The Burning of the Civic Tablets

Rights, legal protections, public accountability tools, and appeal systems are rewritten, restricted, or destroyed. Emergency stewards replace local governance. The Myrmidon Grinders emerge from labor defense and evacuation networks.

## Year 4: The First Hidden Door

Mara Kallix uses compromised smart-city maintenance hardware to open a secure extraction route. The Daedalus Foundry forms around repair, escape infrastructure, anti-surveillance devices, and liberatory tools.

## Year 5: The Funeral Carnival

Rhea Spite and allied street crews transform a commercial concourse into a mass memorial for people erased by the Ledger Purges. The Styx Rats become the Uprising’s culture-jamming and morale cell.

## Year 5: The Thunder Casket Broadcast

The Cabinet of False Olympians turns divine-war authority into televised terror during strikes and scarcity riots caused by the Bronze Gate Levies. The Rats break the spectacle. The cells begin coordinating under shared Underwatch protocols.

## Year 6: The First Descent

Scattered clinics, hackers, defenders, builders, punks, archivists, and signal ghosts begin operating as one underworld resistance. The Dead Index becomes the memory spine of the movement.

## Year 6: Naming of the Chthonic Uprising

The five founding cells accept a shared alliance name: The Chthonic Uprising. The Archivist is recognized as keeper and coordinator, not ruler. The motto is adopted.

**What the surface buries, the underworld remembers.**

## Year 7: Underwatch Protocol

A distributed alert, evidence, care, and mission routing system links the cells. The second motto spreads through dead drops and graffiti.

**No ghost goes uncounted. No god goes unwatched.**
